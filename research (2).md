## Research Question 1: What does evidence-based, survivor-informed anti-trafficking communication actually look like?

### Key Findings

**Survivor-informed means structural inclusion, not tokenism.**
The U.S. Department of State emphasizes that survivors must be included as professionals and experts — not solely as people who share their trauma story. Organizations should "hire and compensate survivors for their expertise" and never engage them "solely to tell the story of their trafficking experience."
— [State.gov – Engaging Survivors of Human Trafficking](https://2021-2025.state.gov/engaging-survivors-of-human-trafficking-2/)

**Consent and story ownership are non-negotiable.**
Before using any survivor's story, organizations must run a robust, informed consent process — being transparent about how and where the story will appear, and allowing the survivor to withdraw consent at any point.
— [State.gov – Engaging Survivors of Human Trafficking](https://2021-2025.state.gov/engaging-survivors-of-human-trafficking-2/)

**Trauma-informed communication reduces re-traumatization.**
The Slavery and Trafficking Survivor Care Standards define trauma-informed communication as techniques that "support professionals to increase the confidence of survivors and minimise the risks of distress and re-traumatisation."
— [Anti-Slavery International – Survivor Care Standards 2018](https://www.antislavery.org/wp-content/uploads/2018/12/Human-Trafficking-Foundation-Care-Standards.pdf)

**Empowerment narratives outperform fear-based messaging.**
A U.S. Department of Labor randomized controlled trial in Nepal found that empowerment narratives are more effective than danger narratives in shifting knowledge and attitudes. Fear-based messaging also risks increasing victim-blaming — one study found that stories describing how individuals became trafficked resulted in an increase in victim-blaming among police.
— [U.S. Dept. of Labor – Nepal RCT Study](https://www.dol.gov/agencies/ilab/reducing-vulnerability-human-trafficking-experimental-intervention-using-anti-0)

**Survivor-informed is a process, not a checkbox.**
The OVC Practical Guide notes that being survivor-informed means intentional survivor input in the design, implementation, and evaluation of anti-trafficking efforts — not a one-time consultation.
— [OVC – Practical Guide: Survivor-Informed Services](https://ovc.ojp.gov/program/human-trafficking/practical-guide-survivor-informed-services.pdf)

### Key Do's and Don'ts for AI-Generated Posts

| ✅ Do | ❌ Don't |
|---|---|
| Use empowerment framing | Use fear or danger narratives |
| Cite verified statistics | Reproduce myths (e.g. "stranger danger") |
| Include a clear, actionable CTA | Make victims seem helpless |
| Use trauma-informed language | Describe graphic details of exploitation |
| Reference hotline resources | Imply survivor stories without consent |

## Research Question 2: What are the legal, ethical, and platform-specific constraints?

### Legal Framework

**FOSTA-SESTA (2018)** holds online platforms legally accountable for content that facilitates sex trafficking. Any awareness tool that inadvertently mimics trafficking recruitment language could be flagged or removed.
— [Hope Against Trafficking](https://www.hopeagainsttrafficking.org/human-trafficking-and-social-media-a-modern-tool-for-exploitation)

**The Trafficking Victims Protection Act (2000)** is the federal baseline for defining and prosecuting trafficking — content should align with its definitions to avoid mischaracterizing trafficking scenarios.
— [State.gov – 2024 TIP Report](https://www.state.gov/reports/2024-trafficking-in-persons-report/)

### Platform-Specific Policies

**TikTok:** Prohibits content that "promotes or facilitates human trafficking or smuggling." Awareness content is allowed but must clearly not glorify or instruct. Credible threats to life are reported to law enforcement; youth sex trafficking content is reported to NCMEC.
— [TikTok Community Guidelines](https://tiktok.com/community-guidelines/en/safety-civility)

**Instagram/Facebook (Meta):** Internal documents revealed that Meta has struggled to enforce its own anti-trafficking policies consistently. A 2021 Polaris report found Facebook recruitment for trafficking increased 125% between 2019–2020. A 2024 U.S. Senate hearing held Meta leadership accountable for exploitation on its platforms.
— [Polaris – Human Trafficking and Social Media](https://polarisproject.org/human-trafficking-and-social-media/)
— [Social Media Victims Law Center](https://socialmediavictims.org/sexual-violence/human-trafficking/)

**Content moderation risk:** Even accurate awareness posts can be incorrectly flagged. Meta's content moderators reported that trafficking-related reports "largely went unheard" and open tickets were "often closed without comment or action."
— [The Exodus Road](https://theexodusroad.com/human-trafficking-and-social-media/)

### Ethical Constraints

- Never use imagery that portrays victims as passive or helpless
- Do not sensationalize or use graphic details — this can re-traumatize survivors who encounter the content
- Avoid "rescue" framing, which has been criticized by survivor advocates
- Always include the **National Human Trafficking Hotline**: 1-888-373-7888 / Text BeFree to 233733

## Research Question 3: How do you measure whether an awareness post works — and avoids harm?

### What Success Looks Like

**KABP shifts (Knowledge, Attitude, Behavior, Practice)** are the gold standard evaluation framework. The U.S. State Dept. confirms that "awareness-raising campaigns can be effective tools in changing knowledge, attitudes, and behaviors regarding human trafficking, but that some changes may be short lived."
— [State.gov – Promising Practices Review](https://2021-2025.state.gov/promising-practices-a-review-of-u-s-government-funded-anti-trafficking-in-persons-programs/)

**Reporting campaigns work like a funnel.** Research shows that hundreds of thousands of people may be targeted, but only a small number will ever be in a position to act on the recommendation. Reach ≠ impact — vanity metrics (likes, shares) are poor proxies for real-world behavior change.
— [ResearchGate – Evaluation as Knowledge Generator](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/325147195_Evaluation_as_knowledge_generator_and_project_improver_Learning_from_demand-side_campaigns_against_trafficking_in_human_beings)

**Pre/post awareness measurement** is a recommended standard. Organizations should measure changes in community awareness through pre- and post-campaign surveys.
— [FundsForNGOs – Anti-Trafficking Metrics](https://us.fundsforngos.org/all-q-and-a/what-metrics-should-i-track-in-a-proposal-for-anti-trafficking-initiatives/)

**Campaigns must include evaluation by design.** The State Dept. TIP Report states: "Anti-trafficking awareness campaigns must include an evaluation component to assess their impact and improve future campaigns."
— [State.gov – TIP Report 2016](https://2009-2017.state.gov/j/tip/rls/tiprpt/2016/258686.htm)

### Measuring Harm (Not Just Impact)

| Risk | What to Watch For |
|---|---|
| Myth amplification | Posts that reinforce "stranger danger" or trafficker stereotypes |
| Victim blaming | Content implying victims "should have known" |
| Stigma against sex workers | Conflating consensual sex work with trafficking |
| Harmful rescue narratives | Framing that encourages vigilante "rescue" actions |
| Re-traumatization | Graphic content that distresses survivors who encounter it |

### Recommended Evaluation Metrics for the Demo

1. **Reach** — impressions, shares (baseline)
2. **Knowledge shift** — pre/post quiz or survey embedded in campaign
3. **Action rate** — hotline calls, website visits, report submissions
4. **Content audit score** — rate each generated post against ethical checklist
5. **Survivor review** — qualitative feedback from survivor advocates
